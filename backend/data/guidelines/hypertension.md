# Dietary Guidelines for Hypertension

The DASH eating plan (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) is a flexible, balanced eating pattern designed to prevent and lower high blood pressure. It requires no special foods and instead provides daily and weekly nutritional goals based on a 2,000-calorie diet.

A core principle of DASH is limiting sodium intake. The standard DASH plan recommends no more than 2,300 milligrams of sodium per day. A lower-sodium version targets 1,500 milligrams per day, which can lower blood pressure even further. Achieving these targets requires reducing processed foods, restaurant meals, cured meats, canned soups, and salty snacks.

The DASH plan emphasizes vegetables, fruits, and whole grains. For a 2,000-calorie diet, the plan recommends 4 to 5 servings of vegetables daily, 4 to 5 servings of fruit daily, and 6 to 8 servings of whole grains daily. These foods provide potassium, magnesium, calcium, fiber, and other nutrients that support healthy blood pressure.

Low-fat or fat-free dairy products are included at 2 to 3 servings per day to provide calcium and protein without excessive saturated fat. Examples include skim milk, low-fat yogurt, and reduced-fat cheese.

The plan includes lean protein sources such as fish, poultry, beans, nuts, and seeds. Meat, poultry, and fish are limited to 6 or fewer ounces per day. Plant proteins like beans, lentils, and nuts are encouraged because they are rich in potassium, magnesium, and fiber.

Foods high in saturated fat should be limited, including fatty meats, full-fat dairy products, and tropical oils such as coconut, palm kernel, and palm oils. Trans fats should be avoided entirely.

Added sugars and sweets are limited to 5 or fewer servings per week. Sugar-sweetened beverages, desserts, and candies contribute excess calories and can displace nutrient-dense foods.

Nuts, seeds, dry beans, and peas are recommended at 4 to 5 servings per week. These provide healthy fats, plant protein, magnesium, and potassium, all of which support cardiovascular health.

Weight management is an important complement to the DASH plan. Even modest weight loss of 5 to 10 percent of body weight can lower blood pressure, especially in people with overweight or obesity.

Alcohol intake should be limited. For most adults, that means no more than one drink per day for women and two drinks per day for men. Excessive alcohol raises blood pressure and can interfere with hypertension medications.

Physical activity also supports blood pressure control. Most adults should aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week plus muscle-strengthening activities on two or more days per week.

Prescribed blood pressure medications should be continued unless a healthcare provider directs otherwise. The DASH eating plan and lifestyle changes work alongside medical treatment.

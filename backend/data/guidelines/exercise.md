# General Exercise Guidelines

Regular physical activity provides significant physical and mental health benefits. For adults, it helps prevent and manage noncommunicable diseases such as cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and certain cancers, while also reducing symptoms of depression and anxiety, improving sleep, and supporting healthy body composition.

Adults aged 18 to 64 should do at least 150 to 300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic physical activity per week, or at least 75 to 150 minutes of vigorous-intensity activity, or an equivalent combination. Activity should be spread throughout the week for optimal benefit.

Muscle-strengthening activities involving major muscle groups should be performed on two or more days per week. Examples include resistance training with weights, resistance bands, bodyweight exercises, or weight-bearing activities that build strength.

Sedentary behavior should be reduced. Adults who sit for long periods should break up sitting time with short bouts of movement, as higher amounts of sedentary time are associated with increased risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and all-cause mortality.

Older adults aged 65 and above should follow the same aerobic and muscle-strengthening recommendations as younger adults, with the addition of multicomponent physical activities that emphasize functional balance and strength training on three or more days per week to prevent falls.

For people who are not currently active, any amount of physical activity is better than none. Starting slowly and progressing gradually reduces injury risk and builds long-term adherence. A reasonable starting point might be 10-minute bouts of walking spread across the day.

Aerobic activities include brisk walking, cycling, swimming, dancing, hiking, and most sports. Vigorous activities include running, fast cycling, swimming laps, and high-intensity interval training. Moderate-intensity activity should raise heart rate and breathing while still allowing conversation.

Flexibility and balance exercises are valuable complements, particularly for older adults and people recovering from injury. Stretching, yoga, tai chi, and balance drills can improve mobility, reduce fall risk, and support recovery.

Rest and recovery days are important. Muscles need time to repair after intense or unaccustomed exercise. A well-designed weekly plan includes a mix of moderate and vigorous days, strength sessions, and lighter active recovery days.

Safety is essential. Individuals should stop exercise and seek medical attention if they experience chest pain, severe shortness of breath, dizziness, fainting, or palpitations. People with known cardiovascular, metabolic, or renal disease should consult a clinician before beginning a vigorous exercise program.

Physical activity should be enjoyable and sustainable. Choosing activities that fit a person's preferences, environment, schedule, and physical abilities increases the likelihood of long-term adherence.

# Behavioral Psychology for Health Behavior Change

Sustainable health behavior change depends on more than knowledge and motivation. The COM-B model proposes that behavior occurs when three components align: Capability, Opportunity, and Motivation. Interventions are most effective when they address the specific barrier that is preventing change.

Capability includes both psychological and physical ability. A person must know what to do and feel confident they can do it. Building skills through small, repeated practice, clear instructions, and just-in-time education increases capability and self-efficacy.

Opportunity refers to the environmental and social factors that make a behavior easier or harder. This includes physical access to healthy food or safe places to exercise, social support from friends and family, and cues in the environment that prompt healthy actions.

Motivation encompasses both automatic impulses and reflective intentions. People are more likely to act when a behavior feels rewarding, aligns with their identity, and is supported by clear goals. Motivation fluctuates, so systems and habits matter more than willpower alone.

Goal setting should follow the SMART framework: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. For example, "walk 20 minutes after lunch on Monday, Wednesday, and Friday" is more actionable than "exercise more."

Implementation intentions, or "if-then" plans, increase follow-through. Examples include: "If it is 7 a.m., then I will do a 10-minute yoga routine" or "If I feel stressed after work, then I will take a 15-minute walk instead of snacking."

Habit stacking links a new behavior to an existing routine. After an established habit such as brushing teeth or pouring morning coffee, a new behavior is added. Over time, the new behavior becomes automatic through repetition and consistent context.

Self-monitoring improves awareness and accountability. Tracking food intake, physical activity, sleep, weight, or blood glucose helps people recognize patterns, celebrate progress, and make data-informed adjustments.

Setbacks are a normal part of behavior change. Self-compassion after a lapse predicts better long-term adherence than self-criticism. The goal is progress over perfection: one missed workout or off-plan meal does not erase previous progress.

Social support strengthens adherence. Having an accountability partner, joining a group, or sharing goals with trusted friends and family increases motivation and makes healthy behaviors more enjoyable.

Rewards and reinforcement should be immediate and meaningful. Intrinsic rewards such as feeling more energetic, sleeping better, or feeling proud are sustainable, but extrinsic rewards such as non-food treats can also help establish new routines.

Environmental design reduces reliance on willpower. Keeping healthy foods visible and accessible, preparing exercise clothes the night before, and removing cues for unwanted behaviors make healthy choices the path of least resistance.

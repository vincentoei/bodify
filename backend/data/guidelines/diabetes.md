# Dietary Guidelines for Diabetes

Evidence-based nutrition therapy is fundamental to diabetes management. The American Diabetes Association emphasizes that there is no single ideal macronutrient distribution for every person with diabetes; instead, recommendations should be individualized based on metabolic goals, preferences, and access.

Carbohydrate intake has the greatest effect on postprandial blood glucose. For many adults with diabetes, a reasonable starting point is 45 to 60 grams of carbohydrate per meal, adjusted based on blood glucose response, activity level, and medication regimen. Distributing carbohydrate intake consistently across meals helps reduce glycemic variability.

The glycemic index and glycemic load of foods can be used as tools to improve post-meal glucose control. Lower glycemic index carbohydrates such as legumes, non-starchy vegetables, intact whole grains, and most fruits are preferred over refined grains, white bread, sugary beverages, and sweets.

Fiber intake should be at least 14 grams per 1,000 calories daily, with emphasis on soluble fiber from oats, beans, lentils, flaxseed, and certain fruits. Higher fiber intake is associated with improved glycemic control, lower cholesterol, and greater satiety.

Protein recommendations are generally 15 to 20 percent of total calories, or roughly 0.8 to 1.0 grams per kilogram of body weight for most adults. Plant-based proteins, fish, poultry, eggs, and lean meats can all fit within a diabetes-friendly eating pattern. People with diabetic kidney disease may need individualized protein targets under clinical supervision.

Dietary fat quality matters more than total fat. Saturated fat should be limited to less than 10 percent of calories, and trans fats should be avoided. Monounsaturated and polyunsaturated fats from olive oil, nuts, seeds, avocado, and fatty fish are encouraged.

Added sugars and sugar-sweetened beverages should be minimized because they cause rapid glucose spikes and contribute to excess calorie intake. Water, sparkling water, unsweetened tea, and coffee are preferred beverages.

Regular physical activity, including both aerobic exercise and resistance training, improves insulin sensitivity and glycemic control. Most adults with diabetes should aim for at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity, spread over at least three days, with no more than two consecutive days without activity.

People using insulin or sulfonylureas should be educated on hypoglycemia prevention, recognition, and treatment, including carrying fast-acting carbohydrate and monitoring glucose around exercise.

Medication management should remain consistent with the prescribing clinician's guidance. Nutrition and lifestyle interventions complement, rather than replace, prescribed diabetes therapies.
